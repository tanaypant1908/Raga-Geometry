Mathematical Notes ▼

What is a pitch-class set? In music theory, a pitch-class set is a collection of notes treated without regard to octave. If you play a C on any piano key, it belongs to the same pitch-class. Here we use 12 pitch-classes (the chromatic scale). A raga is a subset of this universe — typically 5 to 7 notes — with additional rules about how to use them.

What does Jaccard similarity mean? Given two sets A and B, Jaccard = |A ∩ B| / |A ∪ B|. It measures overlap. If Yaman uses {S,R,G,m,P,D,N} and Bhupali uses {S,R,G,P,D}, they share {S,R,G,P,D} = 5 notes, and their union has 7 notes. Jaccard = 5/7 ≈ 71%. This is why the two ragas "sound related" — they share most of their pitch vocabulary.

Why does vadi centrality make mathematical sense? In the directed note graph, the vadi (the most important note) tends to have the highest in-degree + out-degree: more phrases begin and end on it. This mirrors graph-theoretic centrality. The vadi is the "hub" of the raga's grammar — its gravitational center in the network of melodic movement.
